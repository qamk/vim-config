# Common Mistakes by Report Type

## Problem-Solution Reports (Non-Technical)
- ❌ Over-explaining implementation details
- ❌ Using technical jargon without definition
- ❌ Missing customer impact assessment
- ❌ Forgetting to document required permissions or configurations
- ✅ Focus on "what changed" and "how it helps customers"

## Problem-Solution Reports (Developing Engineers)
- ❌ Assuming too much architectural context
- ❌ Not explaining "why" design patterns were chosen
- ❌ Missing code exploration guidance
- ❌ Over-explaining to the point of condescension
- ✅ Balance detail with learning opportunities

## Problem-Solution Reports (Principal+ Engineers)
- ❌ Over-explaining well-known architecture
- ❌ Missing systemic implications
- ❌ Not evaluating alternative approaches
- ❌ Forgetting edge case analysis
- ✅ Focus on architectural implications and root causes

## Problem-Solution Reports (Mixed/Leadership)
- ❌ Too much technical detail obscuring business impact
- ❌ Missing resource or timeline implications
- ❌ Not providing strategic context
- ❌ Forgetting to address cross-functional concerns
- ✅ Balance technical credibility with business clarity

## Analysis Reports
- ❌ Too much implementation detail (assessment, not design)
- ❌ Missing customer impact analysis
- ❌ Not distinguishing high-effort vs. low-effort opportunities
- ❌ Forgetting current workaround analysis
- ✅ Focus on opportunities and strategic recommendations

## Outline Reports (Technical)
- ❌ Not documenting configuration requirements
- ❌ Missing data flow descriptions
- ❌ Forgetting data scoping implications
- ❌ Over-focusing on "what" without "how"
- ✅ Comprehensive technical picture for implementation

## Outline Reports (Non-Technical)
- ❌ Using technical jargon
- ❌ Missing business value statements
- ❌ Not addressing stakeholder perspectives
- ❌ Forgetting to document limitations
- ✅ Focus on business outcomes and user experience

## Post-Mortem Reports
- ❌ Treating the report as blame assignment rather than systemic learning
- ❌ Writing the timeline from memory instead of from logs and git timestamps
- ❌ Omitting positive learnings — purely negative reports discourage future transparency
- ❌ Mixing technical depth into "Narrative" (save it for Post-Mortem)
- ❌ Vague next steps without owners or timeframes ("we should improve testing")
- ❌ Not distinguishing confirmed vs. potential impact in the blast radius
- ❌ Writing the report during active incident response instead of after resolution
- ✅ Focus on systemic improvement, use verified timestamps, credit what worked

## Handoff Reports (Tier 2)
- ❌ Not documenting what was actually tried
- ❌ Vague descriptions like "checked everything"
- ❌ Missing reproduction steps
- ❌ Not explaining why escalation is needed
- ❌ Forgetting to document what was ruled out
- ✅ Be specific about actions taken and findings discovered

## Handoff Reports (Tier 3)
- ❌ Not including actual queries executed
- ❌ Vague statements like "checked the database"
- ❌ Missing file paths when code areas are suspected
- ❌ Not documenting data scoping considerations
- ❌ Forgetting to mention related systems
- ❌ Not including evidence of what was ruled out
- ✅ Be exhaustively specific about technical investigation
